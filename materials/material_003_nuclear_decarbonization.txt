This House would make large-scale nuclear power expansion a central pillar of national electricity decarbonization strategy.
Many governments have pledged to rapidly decarbonize their electricity grids while maintaining affordability, reliability, and industrial competitiveness. In this context, nuclear power has returned to the center of policy debate. Advocates argue that nuclear energy provides dense, low-carbon, dispatchable electricity capable of supporting a modern grid even when sunlight and wind are variable. Opponents respond that although nuclear plants emit little carbon during operation, they are too slow, too expensive, and too politically fragile to serve as the backbone of urgent decarbonization efforts. The dispute is not simply about energy ideology, but about sequencing, risk tolerance, and state capacity.
The strongest case for nuclear expansion begins with reliability. Wind and solar generation have become dramatically cheaper in many regions, but their output depends on weather, geography, and time of day. Grid operators can smooth some of this variability through transmission, storage, demand management, and diversified renewable portfolios, yet many systems still struggle with extended periods of low wind or high evening demand. Nuclear plants can run for long periods at high capacity factors, providing a stable low-carbon floor under the grid. For countries with energy-intensive manufacturing, winter heating demand, or limited domestic fossil resources, that stability can be strategically valuable.
Nuclear advocates also stress land use, fuel security, and long-term system resilience. A relatively small amount of uranium can generate a large amount of electricity, and stockpiles can often be maintained more easily than fossil fuel imports. Some supporters argue that a grid built around firm low-carbon generation reduces dependence on vast overbuilding of renewables, extremely large storage deployments, or politically difficult transmission corridors. In this telling, nuclear is not the enemy of renewables but the complement that makes a deeply decarbonized grid more credible.
The counterargument turns on time and cost. Large reactors in liberalized electricity markets have repeatedly suffered from delays, cost overruns, financing crises, and changing safety requirements. Decarbonization is a race against the clock, and critics argue that every dollar and every year committed to a new reactor is a dollar and a year not spent on cleaner technologies that can scale faster right now, such as solar, wind, transmission upgrades, grid batteries, industrial efficiency, and heat electrification. Even if a completed reactor is useful, a project that comes online a decade late may have crowded out more immediate emissions reductions.
Risk and public legitimacy also matter. Nuclear accidents are rare, but their potential consequences are politically and psychologically outsized. Long-term waste management remains unresolved in many countries, and local communities often resist hosting either reactors or waste repositories. Supporters answer that fossil fuels already impose enormous health and climate harms that are normalized rather than spectacular, and that modern reactor designs incorporate substantial safety improvements. Still, nuclear politics operates under a legitimacy burden that wind, solar, and efficiency measures often do not face in the same way.
There is also an institutional capability question. Some countries with existing nuclear workforces, strong regulators, state-backed utilities, and prior reactor fleets may be able to expand nuclear capacity more effectively than countries starting nearly from scratch. For the latter group, building a central decarbonization strategy around nuclear may require recreating supply chains, training engineers, establishing regulatory expertise, and securing public consent under highly polarized conditions. What looks like a realistic backbone in one national context may be an unrealistic distraction in another.
Small modular reactors are often introduced as a possible compromise. Their supporters claim factory manufacturing, standardized designs, and smaller project sizes could eventually reduce cost and construction risk. Skeptics note that most such designs remain commercially immature, and that betting central strategy on technologies not yet proven at scale may simply reproduce the delays and disappointment associated with earlier nuclear promises. The question is whether these designs should be viewed as an emerging opportunity or as a reason to remain cautious.
Ultimately, making nuclear expansion a central pillar of decarbonization requires more than believing nuclear has value. It requires confidence that the state can deliver projects on time, finance them responsibly, regulate them competently, and maintain public trust while competing pathways are available. The real policy choice is not between decarbonization and anti-decarbonization, but between rival portfolios of speed, firmness, capital intensity, political risk, and technological learning.
